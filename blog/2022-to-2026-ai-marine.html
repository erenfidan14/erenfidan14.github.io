From 2022 to 2026: How AI Changed Marine Engineering (And My Predictions)

In 2022, I was a research assistant at Aalto working on "Emerging Methods in Seakeeping." I predicted AI/ML would revolutionize CFD and ship design. Four years later, here's what actually happened, what I got right, and what surprised me.

It feels strange to write this in 2026, looking back at my 2022 self‚Äîa third-year bachelor student at Istanbul Technical University, spending my summer at Aalto University in Espoo, Finland. I was working on a research report titled "Emerging Methods in Seakeeping: Bibliometric Analysis, Systematic Literature Review, and Future Research Directions."

Back then, I was convinced that AI and machine learning would completely transform naval architecture within 3-5 years. I wrote about deep learning for ship motion prediction, neural networks for automatic berthing, and digital twins becoming standard in ship design. Some of it happened. Some didn't. And some things I never saw coming.

What I Predicted in 2022

My 2022 report identified several key trends I expected to mature by 2025-2026:

  1. Deep Learning for Ship Motion Prediction: I thought LSTM neural networks would replace traditional potential flow theory for real-time seakeeping predictions.
  2. AI-Accelerated CFD: I predicted machine learning would reduce CFD simulation times from days to hours through surrogate models.
  3. Digital Twins: I expected every major shipyard to have digital twin capabilities for real-time performance monitoring.
  4. Reinforcement Learning: I wrote about DRL (Deep Reinforcement Learning) being used for autonomous ship maneuvering within 5 years.

What Actually Happened (2026 Reality)

üéØ 2022 Predictions

  • AI replaces traditional seakeeping methods
  • CFD simulation time: hours instead of days
  • Digital twins become industry standard
  • Autonomous ships using DRL by 2026
  • Cloud-based ship design platforms

‚úÖ 2026 Reality

  • AI complements (not replaces) traditional methods
  • CFD: 30-40% faster with ML surrogates
  • Digital twins: 15% of newbuilds (not standard)
  • Autonomous ships: still in trials
  • Cloud design: adopted by major yards

‚úÖ What I Got Right

Cloud-based design platforms did become mainstream. During my time at Turkish Airlines Technic in 2024, I saw how aviation was adopting cloud workflows, and shipbuilding followed. The pandemic accelerated this, but the shift stuck.

ML-accelerated CFD is real, but not as revolutionary as I expected. In marine CFD, ML surrogates and data-driven components are useful, but the best gains often come from workflow optimization, meshing automation, and HPC improvements‚Äînot only models.

‚ùå What I Got Wrong

Deep learning didn't replace traditional methods. In 2022, I thought neural networks would make strip theory obsolete. Instead, they coexist. The physics-based models are still more reliable for certification, while ML handles optimization and real-time predictions. The marine industry is conservative‚Äîand for good reason when safety is involved.

Autonomous ships are further away than I thought. DRL for ship maneuvering works in simulations, but real-world deployment is slowed by regulation, liability, and insurance. The technology progresses faster than the governance.

üöÄ What Surprised Me

üí° The Big Surprise: Generative AI for Design

I never mentioned LLMs or generative AI in my 2022 report. ChatGPT didn't exist when I wrote it. Yet generative AI has become a major disruptor‚Äînot by replacing CFD, but by speeding up the design workflow: CAD copilots, automated documentation, and natural-language interfaces for simulation tools.

Another surprise: the talent shift. In 2022, I wrote about the need for naval architects to learn data science. By 2026, the reverse is also true‚Äîtech teams hire domain engineers to build maritime AI.

What This Means for 2030

Based on what I've learned between 2022 and 2026, here are my new predictions:

  1. Hybrid workflows will dominate. AI + physics-based models, not AI replacing physics. The "digital thread" connecting design, build, and operations will matter more than any single model.
  2. Generative design will mature. By 2030, AI will generate candidate hull forms optimized for routes and operational profiles, but humans will validate, certify, and own responsibility.
  3. Real-time decision support. Not fully autonomous ships, but AI-assisted route and operations tools for captains and fleets in real time.
  4. Standardization of digital twins. Class societies will push standards that make twins as normal as stability documentation.

Personal Reflection

Reading my 2022 report today is humbling. I was enthusiastic but naive about adoption timelines. The technology moved fast; the industry moved slow. But shipbuilding is safety-critical; deliberate validation is rational.

"The future is already here‚Äîit's just not evenly distributed." ‚Äî William Gibson

Working at Turkish Airlines Technic taught me that aviation often adopts faster than maritime, but both move toward the same goal: data-driven, optimized, sustainable operations.

Now in 2026, finishing my MSc at Aalto with an Excellence Scholarship, I'm more optimistic than ever‚Äîjust with more realistic expectations. The marine industry doesn't need disruption; it needs integration of new tools into proven workflows.

If you're reading this in 2030, check back. I suspect I'll have more surprises to report.
